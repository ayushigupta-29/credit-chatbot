# Loans and Credit

## Types of Loans in India

### Secured Loans
Backed by collateral — an asset the lender can seize if you default.

- **Home Loan:** Largest loan most people take. Property is the collateral. Tenure up to 30 years. Lowest interest rates (8–10% p.a.) because risk to lender is low.
- **Car Loan / Auto Loan:** Vehicle is the collateral. Tenure 1–7 years. Rates typically 7–12% p.a.
- **Loan Against Property (LAP):** You mortgage your property for a large loan. Used for business or personal needs.
- **Loan Against FD:** You borrow against your Fixed Deposit. Very low rates (1–2% above FD rate). Minimal credit score impact since it's fully secured.
- **Gold Loan:** Borrow against physical gold. Fast disbursal, minimal documentation, and credit score is often not a primary criterion since the gold is collateral. Gold loans are common and widely accepted, particularly in South India. However, if a credit report shows a gold loan alongside other stress signals — missed payments, high utilisation, multiple enquiries — lenders may interpret the combination as financial distress. The gold loan itself is not a red flag; the context around it matters.

### Unsecured Loans
No collateral required. Lenders rely entirely on your creditworthiness.

- **Personal Loan:** For any purpose — medical, travel, wedding, etc. Rates 10–24% p.a. depending on profile. Tenure 1–5 years.
- **Business Loan:** For business expenses. Rates vary widely based on business vintage, turnover, and credit profile.
- **Education Loan:** For higher education. Some have government interest subsidies under schemes like the Central Scheme for Interest Subsidy (CSIS).
- **Consumer Durable Loan / Buy on EMI:** For purchasing appliances, electronics, and other consumer goods. Often offered at point of sale — at a store or on e-commerce platforms like Amazon or Flipkart. Comes in two forms:
  - **Zero-cost EMI:** The product price is split into monthly instalments with no explicit interest. In practice, the discount you would have received is adjusted into the price — so you are effectively paying interest, just not visibly.
  - **Standard EMI:** Interest is charged explicitly, often at 12–24% p.a.
- **Buy Now Pay Later (BNPL):** A short-term credit product that lets you purchase immediately and pay later — typically in 15 days, 30 days, or split across 3–6 instalments. Offered by fintechs like LazyPay, Simpl, ZestMoney, and also embedded in platforms like Flipkart Pay Later and Amazon Pay Later. BNPL products are reported to credit bureaus, so missed BNPL payments affect your credit score just like a missed loan EMI.

## How Loans Impact Your Credit Score

### When You Apply (Hard Enquiry)
Every loan application triggers a hard enquiry on your credit report. This:
- Temporarily drops your score by a few points
- Stays visible on your report for 2 years
- Multiple applications in a short time is a red flag for lenders

**Smart tip:** Use loan eligibility checkers (soft enquiry tools) on platforms like Paisabazaar or BankBazaar before applying. These show you likely outcomes without affecting your score.

### When Your Loan is Approved and Running
- A new loan increases your total debt, which can slightly lower your score initially
- Regular on-time EMI payments are reported monthly to bureaus and build your score steadily
- A mix of loan types (secured + unsecured) improves your credit mix factor

### When You Close a Loan
- A successfully closed loan is one of the strongest positive signals on a credit report
- It shows you borrowed, managed the obligation responsibly, and repaid in full
- Closed loans remain visible on your report for 7 years (per CICRA 2005 and RBI guidelines) — they continue to positively contribute during this period

### If You Miss an EMI
- Lenders report payment data to bureaus on a monthly cycle. A payment that is overdue can appear on your report at the very next monthly update — there is no guaranteed 30-day grace period before it is reported.
- A missed payment can drop your score significantly. The exact impact varies by bureau, your current score, and your overall credit profile — a person with a high score (780+) typically sees a larger absolute drop than someone already in the lower range.
- Consistently missed payments lead to the account being classified as NPA (Non-Performing Asset) — this happens when a payment is overdue by 90 days or more (90 DPD — Days Past Due).
- NPA status severely damages your score and is retained on your report for 7 years from the date of last activity, per RBI/CICRA guidelines.

## Loan Settlements — What You Must Know
A loan settlement happens when you negotiate with the lender to pay less than the full outstanding amount, and the lender agrees to close the account.

**Why people do it:** Financial hardship — they cannot repay the full amount.

**The credit score consequence:**
- The account is marked as "Settled" on your credit report — a significant negative marker
- "Settled" signals to future lenders that you did not honour the full debt
- This stays on your report for 7 years from the date of settlement (per CICRA 2005 guidelines)
- Many lenders will decline applications during this period

**Better alternatives to settlement:**
1. Request a loan restructuring or moratorium from your lender
2. Negotiate an EMI reduction rather than a settlement
3. Use a debt consolidation loan to manage multiple dues
Always try to close loans in full. Settlement should be the absolute last resort.

## Repayment Structures — EMI, EDI, and More

### EMI (Equated Monthly Instalment)
The most common repayment structure. A fixed amount paid every month that includes both interest and principal repayment.

**EMI Formula:**
EMI = P × r × (1+r)^n / ((1+r)^n - 1)
Where:
- P = Principal loan amount
- r = Monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12)
- n = Number of monthly instalments

**Key behaviour:** In the early months of a loan, most of your EMI goes toward paying interest, and very little reduces the principal. As the loan matures, this flips — more goes to principal and less to interest. This is called an amortisation schedule.

### EDI (Equated Daily Instalment)
A repayment structure where instalments are collected daily instead of monthly. Common in:
- Short-term business loans and merchant cash advances
- Microfinance loans
- Some MSME lending products from fintechs

**Why lenders use it:** Daily collections reduce default risk for lenders and align with businesses that generate daily revenue (retail shops, vendors, etc.).

**Impact on borrower:** The total interest paid is similar to an EMI loan at the same rate, but the daily deduction means you always need to maintain a minimum balance. Missing a daily collection is treated the same as missing any payment and gets reported to bureaus.

### Bullet Repayment
The full principal is repaid in one lump sum at the end of the loan tenure, with interest paid periodically or also at the end. Common in loans against FD and some business loans.

### Flexi Loans
A hybrid product where you are given a credit limit, you draw down as needed, and pay interest only on the amount used. Repayments go back into the limit, which can be drawn again. Similar to a line of credit. Common in personal flexi loans offered by fintechs and some NBFCs.

## Flat Rate vs Reducing Balance Rate — Critical Difference

This is one of the most misunderstood aspects of loans and directly impacts how much you actually pay.

### Flat Interest Rate
Interest is calculated on the original principal for the entire tenure, regardless of how much you have repaid.

**Example:** ₹1,00,000 loan at 10% flat for 2 years.
- Annual interest = ₹1,00,000 × 10% = ₹10,000
- Total interest for 2 years = ₹20,000
- Total repayment = ₹1,20,000
- Monthly EMI = ₹1,20,000 ÷ 24 = ₹5,000

Even though you are paying back principal every month, you are being charged interest on the full ₹1,00,000 throughout. This is costly to the borrower.

### Reducing Balance Rate (Diminishing Balance)
Interest is calculated only on the outstanding principal remaining after each payment. As you repay, the principal reduces, and so does the interest charged each month.

**Example:** ₹1,00,000 loan at 10% reducing balance for 2 years.
- EMI = ₹4,615 (using the EMI formula)
- Total repayment = ₹4,615 × 24 = ₹1,10,760
- Total interest = ₹10,760

**The difference:** Same loan, same stated rate — but flat rate costs ₹20,000 in interest while reducing balance costs only ₹10,760. The flat rate is almost double the effective cost.

**Rule of thumb:** A flat rate of 10% is roughly equivalent to a reducing balance rate of ~18–19%. Always ask lenders which method they use and convert to reducing balance for fair comparison.

Consumer durable loans and some personal loans are often quoted in flat rates to make the rate sound lower. Always ask for the equivalent reducing balance rate.

## Key Loan Terms — Complete Glossary

**ROI (Rate of Interest):** The basic interest rate charged on the loan. Can be flat or reducing balance. Does not include fees or other charges.

**APR (Annual Percentage Rate):** A more complete cost measure than ROI. Includes the interest rate plus all mandatory fees — processing fees, insurance, administrative charges. Expressed as an annual percentage. Two loans with the same ROI can have very different APRs if one has higher fees. Always compare APR, not just ROI.

**IRR (Internal Rate of Return):** In lending, IRR represents the true annualised cost of the loan to the borrower, accounting for all cash flows — disbursement, all repayments, fees, and timing. IRR is the most accurate measure of the true cost of a loan. Lenders use IRR internally; borrowers should ask for it when comparing loan products.

**Processing Fee:** A one-time fee charged by the lender for processing your loan application. Typically 0.5–3% of the loan amount. This is deducted upfront from the disbursed amount.

**Prepayment / Foreclosure Penalty:** A charge for paying off your loan before the agreed tenure ends. Two types:
- **Flat penalty:** A percentage of the outstanding principal (typically 2–5%)
- **Time-dependent penalty:** The lender charges a penalty only if you close the loan before a certain number of months (e.g., no prepayment allowed in the first 6 or 12 months; after that, 2% of outstanding principal). This protects the lender's minimum expected interest income.
Always read the prepayment terms before taking a loan — the interest saved by prepaying almost always outweighs a percentage penalty, but time-lock conditions can restrict your flexibility.

**Moratorium / Holiday Period:** A period at the start of the loan (sometimes 1–6 months) during which you do not pay EMIs. Interest continues to accrue during this period. Common in education loans and project-based business loans.

**LTV (Loan to Value):** The ratio of the loan amount to the value of the asset being used as collateral. Example: Home worth ₹50 lakh, loan of ₹40 lakh — LTV = 80%. RBI caps LTV for home loans at 75–90% depending on the loan size.

**DPD (Days Past Due):** The number of days a payment is overdue. Shown on your credit report. 0 DPD = paid on time. 30 DPD, 60 DPD, 90 DPD are progressively more serious. At 90 DPD, the account is classified as NPA.

**NPA (Non-Performing Asset):** Classification given to a loan account where repayment is overdue by 90 days or more. An NPA classification is one of the most serious negative events on a credit report.

**FOIR (Fixed Obligation to Income Ratio):** Total monthly loan EMIs divided by monthly income. Most lenders want this below 40–50% before approving a new loan.

**Co-applicant / Guarantor:** A second person who signs the loan agreement and is equally liable for repayment. Adding a co-applicant with a strong credit profile can improve approval chances. If the primary borrower defaults, the co-applicant's credit score is equally impacted.

**Sanction Letter:** A formal document from the lender confirming loan approval, stating the amount, interest rate, tenure, and all terms. Issued before disbursement.

**Disbursement:** The actual transfer of the loan amount to the borrower's account.

## Loan Eligibility — What Lenders Look At
When you apply for a loan, lenders evaluate:

1. **Credit Score** — minimum 700–750 for most lenders
2. **Income** — stable, sufficient to service the EMI
3. **Employment Type** — salaried borrowers are generally considered lower risk than self-employed. Self-employed borrowers often face higher interest rates and stricter documentation requirements because their income is less predictable.
4. **Existing Obligations (FOIR)** — total EMIs as a % of monthly income. Most lenders want this below 40–50%.
5. **Age** — loan tenure cannot extend beyond retirement age (typically 60–65 for salaried, 70 for self-employed in some cases)
6. **Relationship with lender** — existing customers often receive preferential rates and faster processing
