# Improving Your Credit Score

## How Long Does It Take to Improve a Credit Score?
Credit improvement is not instant. It is a gradual process that reflects consistent financial behaviour over time. Here are realistic timelines:

| Action | Time to See Impact |
|---|---|
| Paying overdue bills | 1–3 months |
| Reducing credit utilisation | 1–2 months (after statement cycle) |
| Removing errors from report | 30–45 days (after dispute resolution) |
| Building credit history from scratch | 6–12 months |
| Recovering from a default or settlement | 2–7 years |

There is no shortcut. Anyone promising a quick credit score fix is likely a scam.

## Step-by-Step Action Plan to Improve Your Score

### Step 1: Get Your Credit Report First
Before improving your score, you need to know where you stand and why.
- Get your free credit report from CIBIL, Experian, CRIF, or Equifax
- Look for: missed payments, high utilisation, errors, unknown accounts
- Errors are more common than people think — always verify

### Step 2: Pay All Overdue Amounts Immediately
If you have any overdue EMIs or credit card bills, paying them is the single highest-impact action you can take.
- Even partial payment is better than nothing
- Lenders report payment status to bureaus monthly — so a paid account will reflect within 30–60 days
- "Settled" is better than "written off" but worse than "closed" — always try to pay in full

### Step 3: Never Miss a Payment Going Forward
Set up auto-pay for at least the minimum amount due on all credit cards and EMIs. Missing even one payment can drop your score by 50–100 points.
- Auto-pay the minimum to protect your score
- Then manually pay the full amount to avoid interest

### Step 4: Reduce Your Credit Utilisation
If your utilisation is above 30%, bring it down.
- Pay down your credit card balance before the statement date (not just the due date)
- Request a credit limit increase from your lender — same spending becomes lower utilisation
- Spread spending across multiple cards if you have them

### Step 5: Do Not Close Old Credit Cards
Your oldest credit card contributes positively to your credit age. Closing it:
- Reduces your total available credit (increases utilisation)
- Reduces your average account age
- Both of these lower your score

Keep old cards alive with small occasional transactions.

### Step 6: Limit New Credit Applications
Every loan or credit card application triggers a hard enquiry. Space applications at least 3–6 months apart. If you need to compare loan offers, do it within a 2-week window — bureaus treat multiple enquiries in a short period as a single enquiry.

### Step 7: Build a Credit Mix Over Time
If you only have credit cards, consider a small personal loan or a secured loan (like a loan against FD). A healthy mix of secured and unsecured credit signals financial maturity.

## Special Situations

### If You Have Never Had Credit (No Credit History)
Lenders are reluctant to give credit to people with no history — the classic chicken-and-egg problem. Solutions:
- **Secured credit card:** You deposit money with the lender (say ₹20,000) and get a credit card with that limit. Use it, pay on time, and build history.
- **Credit-builder loan:** Some fintechs offer small loans specifically to build credit history.
- **Become an authorised user:** Ask a family member with good credit to add you to their credit card account.

### If You Have a Default or Settlement on Your Record
- A default (loan not paid) stays on your report for 7 years
- A settlement (paid less than full amount) stays for 7 years and is viewed negatively by lenders
- The only way out is time + consistent good behaviour going forward
- Some lenders will still work with you if the default is old (3+ years) and recent history is clean

### If You Have Errors on Your Credit Report
You have the right to dispute errors with the bureau. Common errors:
- Loans you never took (identity fraud or data error)
- Accounts marked as active when they are closed
- Wrong personal details
- Payments marked as missed when they were made on time

File a dispute directly on the bureau's website. They are legally required to resolve it within 30 days.

## What NOT to Do

- **Do not pay a "credit repair" company** — anything they do legally, you can do yourself for free
- **Do not close multiple credit cards at once** — sudden drop in available credit hurts your score
- **Do not apply for credit multiple times in a short period** — multiple hard enquiries are a red flag
- **Do not ignore small outstanding amounts** — even a ₹500 unpaid amount can be reported and hurt your score
