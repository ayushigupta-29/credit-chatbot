# Credit Cards

## What is a Credit Card?
A credit card is a financial instrument that lets you borrow money from a lender up to a pre-approved limit to make purchases. You pay back what you borrowed either in full at the end of the billing cycle (interest-free) or in instalments (with interest).

Unlike a debit card, which uses your own money, a credit card uses the lender's money — making how you use it a significant factor in your credit score.

## Credit Card as a Zero-Interest Short-Term Loan
If you need short-term credit at zero interest, a credit card is the best instrument available. Here's why:

- Every billing cycle is 30 days. Your payment due date is typically 15–20 days after the statement date.
- This means if you make a purchase at the start of a billing cycle, you effectively get up to **45–50 days of completely interest-free credit** — from the day of purchase to the payment due date.
- As long as you pay the full outstanding amount by the due date, you pay absolutely zero interest.
- No personal loan, no BNPL product, and no other credit instrument offers this with zero cost.

**Example:** You buy a laptop worth ₹80,000 on the 1st of the month. Your statement is generated on the 30th. Your due date is the 15th of the next month. You've had free use of ₹80,000 for 45 days at zero cost.

This makes credit cards a powerful cash flow management tool for people who are disciplined about paying in full every month.

## Key Terms You Must Know

**Credit Limit:** The maximum amount you can spend on your card. Set by the lender based on your income and credit score.

**Billing Cycle:** The monthly period (usually 30 days) for which your spending is tracked. At the end of each cycle, a statement is generated.

**Statement Date:** The date your monthly statement is generated, showing total amount due.

**Payment Due Date:** The date by which you must pay. Typically 15–20 days after the statement date.

**Minimum Amount Due (MAD):** The minimum payment required to keep your account active and avoid a late payment fee — usually 5% of the outstanding balance. Paying only the MAD means you carry a balance and accrue interest.

**Grace Period:** The interest-free window between your statement date and payment due date. If you pay in full before the due date, you pay zero interest.

**Annual Percentage Rate (APR):** The annual interest rate on your credit card. In India, credit card interest rates range from 24% to 48% per annum — among the highest interest rates in any financial product.

## How Credit Utilisation Works
Credit utilisation is your credit card balance as a percentage of your credit limit.

**Formula:** (Amount Spent ÷ Credit Limit) × 100

**Example:** Limit ₹1,00,000 — Spent ₹35,000 — Utilisation = 35%

**Why it matters for your score:**
- Below 30%: Ideal, viewed positively by bureaus
- 30%–50%: Acceptable but starting to signal stress
- Above 50%: Negative signal, will lower your score
- Above 75%: Significant negative impact

**Important:** Bureaus capture your utilisation on your statement date, not your due date. So if you spend heavily mid-cycle and pay before the due date, the high utilisation is still recorded. To keep utilisation low, pay down your balance before your statement date.

## Secured vs Unsecured Credit Cards

**Unsecured Credit Card (regular):**
- No collateral required
- Issued based on your income and credit score
- Higher limits, better rewards
- Requires an existing credit history to get approved

**Secured Credit Card:**
- Backed by a Fixed Deposit (FD) you place with the lender
- Your credit limit is typically 80–90% of your FD amount
- Ideal for people with no credit history or poor credit
- Works exactly like a regular card — use it, pay on time, build history
- After 12–18 months of good behaviour, lenders often upgrade to unsecured

## How Applying for a Credit Card Affects Your Score
Every credit card application triggers a **hard enquiry** — the lender pulls your credit report to evaluate you. This:
- Temporarily reduces your score by 5–10 points
- Stays on your credit report for 2 years
- Multiple applications in a short time signal financial desperation to lenders

**Smart approach:** Research cards thoroughly, pick one that matches your profile, apply once. Don't spray applications across multiple lenders.

## Tips for Using Credit Cards Wisely

1. **Pay the full amount due every month**, not just the minimum. The interest rate (24–48% p.a.) makes carrying a balance extremely expensive.

2. **Set up auto-pay for the full amount** — not just the minimum — so you never miss a payment.

3. **Use your card regularly but lightly** — small transactions paid in full build credit history without risk.

4. **Don't use your card for cash withdrawals** — cash advances have no grace period, charge a transaction fee (2–3%), and start accruing interest immediately.

5. **Keep your oldest card active** — even one small purchase per month keeps it alive and protects your credit age.

6. **Review your statement monthly** — look for unauthorised transactions immediately.

## Rewards, Cashback, and Points — Are They Worth It?
Credit card rewards (cashback, travel points, lounge access) are genuine benefits — but only if you pay in full every month. If you carry a balance and pay 36% annual interest to earn 1% cashback, you are losing money. Rewards are only valuable when you use credit as a payment tool, not as a borrowing tool.

## Credit Cards and Your Credit Score: Summary
| Action | Impact on Score |
|---|---|
| Paying full amount on time | Very Positive |
| Paying minimum amount only | Neutral (avoids penalty, but carries balance) |
| Missing payment | Very Negative |
| Low utilisation (< 30%) | Positive |
| High utilisation (> 50%) | Negative |
| Applying for a new card | Slight temporary dip |
| Closing an old card | Negative (reduces credit age and limit) |
