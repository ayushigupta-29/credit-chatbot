# Frequently Asked Questions — Credit

## Credit Score Basics

**Q: What is a good credit score in India?**
A score of 750 and above is generally considered good. Most lenders approve loans comfortably at this level and offer competitive interest rates. Scores above 800 are excellent and give you the most negotiating power with lenders.

**Q: Why do I have different scores on different platforms?**
Because India has four credit bureaus — CIBIL, Experian, CRIF High Mark, and Equifax — and each calculates scores using their own algorithm. The underlying data (your payment history, loans, enquiries) is the same, but the weightage applied differs slightly. All four scores are valid. Different lenders may pull from different bureaus.

**Q: Does checking my own credit score lower it?**
No. Checking your own score is called a soft enquiry and has absolutely no impact on your score. Only hard enquiries — when a lender checks your report after you apply for credit — affect your score. Check your score as often as you like.

**Q: How often is my credit score updated?**
Lenders report your payment and account data to bureaus typically once a month. Your score is recalculated each time new data comes in. So changes in your financial behaviour reflect within 30–45 days.

**Q: I have never taken a loan or credit card. What is my credit score?**
You likely have no credit score at all — sometimes called NH (No History) or NA (Not Applicable). This is not the same as a bad score, but it is a problem because lenders have no data to evaluate you. The solution is to start building credit: get a secured credit card, use it for small purchases, and pay in full every month.

## Credit Cards

**Q: Should I pay the minimum amount due or the full amount?**
Always pay the full amount. Paying only the minimum keeps your account active and avoids a late fee, but the remaining balance accrues interest at 24–48% per annum — one of the highest rates of any financial product. The minimum due is a trap that keeps you in revolving debt.

**Q: Is a credit card a good way to get short-term credit at zero cost?**
Yes — if used correctly. If you make a purchase at the start of your billing cycle, you get up to 45–50 days of completely interest-free credit (from purchase date to payment due date). As long as you pay the full outstanding amount by the due date, you pay zero interest. No other credit product offers this.

**Q: Will closing my credit card improve my score?**
Usually no — it often does the opposite. Closing a card reduces your total available credit limit (increasing utilisation) and may reduce your average credit account age. Both can lower your score. Unless there is a specific reason (high annual fee, fraud risk), keep old cards open with occasional small usage.

**Q: What happens if I miss a credit card payment?**
A late payment fee is charged immediately. The missed payment is reported to bureaus at the next monthly reporting cycle and shows as a DPD (Days Past Due) on your report. Your score will drop. If unpaid for 90+ days, the account is classified as NPA. Always pay at least the minimum due to avoid the worst consequences.

**Q: What is the difference between a credit card and a debit card?**
A debit card uses your own money — it draws directly from your bank account. A credit card uses the lender's money up to a pre-approved limit, which you repay later. Only credit card usage is reported to credit bureaus and affects your credit score. Debit card usage has no impact on your credit profile.

## Loans

**Q: How many loans can I have at the same time?**
There is no legal limit. However, every active loan adds to your FOIR (Fixed Obligation to Income Ratio). Most lenders want your total EMIs to stay below 40–50% of your monthly income. Beyond that, new loan applications are likely to be declined regardless of your credit score.

**Q: Does prepaying a loan help my credit score?**
Not significantly in terms of score, but it saves you money on interest. A fully closed loan does appear positively on your report. Be aware of prepayment penalties — some lenders impose a lock-in period (e.g., no prepayment in the first 6–12 months) or charge a penalty of 2–5% of the outstanding amount.

**Q: What is the difference between a loan settlement and loan closure?**
Loan closure means you repaid the full outstanding amount — the account shows as "Closed" on your report, which is positive. Loan settlement means you and the lender agreed to close the account for less than the full amount owed — the account shows as "Settled," which is a significant negative marker and stays on your report for 7 years.

**Q: What is a flat interest rate vs a reducing balance rate?**
A flat rate calculates interest on the original loan amount throughout the tenure. A reducing balance rate calculates interest only on the remaining outstanding principal, which decreases as you repay. At the same stated rate, a flat rate costs significantly more — roughly double. A 10% flat rate is approximately equivalent to an 18–19% reducing balance rate. Always ask lenders which method they use and compare on reducing balance terms.

**Q: What does a lender mean by APR vs ROI?**
ROI (Rate of Interest) is just the interest rate on the loan. APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the total cost of the loan expressed annually — it includes ROI plus all mandatory fees like processing fees, insurance, and administrative charges. APR is a more accurate comparison tool. Two loans with the same ROI can have very different APRs if one has higher fees.

## Credit Reports

**Q: How do I get my free credit report?**
Every Indian is entitled to one free credit report per year from each of the four bureaus — CIBIL, Experian, CRIF, and Equifax. Visit their websites directly. You can also check your score anytime on apps like Bachatt, CRED, BankBazaar, or Paisabazaar.

**Q: I found an error on my credit report. What do I do?**
Raise a dispute directly on the bureau's website. Provide documentation supporting your claim (bank statement, loan closure letter, etc.). The bureau is legally required to investigate and respond within 30 days. If the lender confirms it was an error, it will be corrected. You can escalate to the RBI Ombudsman if unresolved.

**Q: Can a credit repair company remove bad history from my report?**
No legitimate company can remove accurate negative information from your credit report. Only errors can be disputed and corrected. Anyone claiming otherwise is running a scam. Save your money — the dispute process is free and you can do it yourself.

**Q: How long does negative information stay on my report?**
Under CICRA 2005 and RBI guidelines, credit information is retained for 7 years from the date of last activity on the account. This includes defaults, settlements, and NPA classifications. After 7 years it ages off automatically.

## Building Credit from Scratch

**Q: I am new to credit. Where do I start?**
Start with a secured credit card — deposit an amount with a lender (say ₹20,000–₹50,000) and get a card with that as your limit. Use it for small regular purchases (groceries, subscriptions) and pay the full amount every month. After 12–18 months of consistent behaviour, you will have a good credit score and can apply for unsecured products.

**Q: How long does it take to build a credit score from zero?**
You need at least 6 months of credit history for bureaus to generate a score. A score in the good range (700+) typically takes 12–18 months of consistent, responsible behaviour. There are no shortcuts — time and discipline are the only ingredients.

**Q: Does my income affect my credit score?**
No. Your income is not a factor in your credit score calculation. A person earning ₹20,000 a month with perfect payment history can have a higher score than someone earning ₹2,00,000 who misses payments. Income affects your loan eligibility (how much a lender will give you) but not your score itself.
