# Credit Score Fundamentals

## What is a Credit Score?
A credit score is a three-digit number that represents your creditworthiness — how likely you are to repay borrowed money on time. In India, credit scores range from 300 to 900. The higher your score, the more trustworthy you appear to lenders.

Lenders — banks, NBFCs, and fintech companies — use your credit score to decide:
- Whether to approve your loan or credit card application
- What interest rate to offer you (higher score = lower interest rate)
- How much credit limit to extend

## Credit Bureaus in India
India has four RBI-licensed credit bureaus that calculate and maintain credit scores:

1. **CIBIL (TransUnion CIBIL)** — the most widely used bureau in India. Most people refer to their "CIBIL score" generically, but it specifically refers to TransUnion CIBIL's score.
2. **Experian** — widely used by banks and NBFCs
3. **CRIF High Mark** — commonly used for microfinance and rural lending
4. **Equifax** — used by several large banks

Each bureau calculates your score slightly differently based on their own algorithm, so your score may vary slightly across bureaus. All four are equally valid.

## Credit Score Ranges in India
| Score Range | Rating | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 750 – 900 | Excellent | Easy loan approvals, best interest rates |
| 700 – 749 | Good | Most loans approved, competitive rates |
| 650 – 699 | Fair | Loans possible but at higher interest rates |
| 600 – 649 | Poor | Limited options, may need collateral |
| 300 – 599 | Very Poor | Most applications rejected |

A score of 750+ is generally considered the benchmark for good credit health in India.

## What Makes Up Your Credit Score?
Your score is calculated based on five main factors:

### 1. Payment History (35% weightage)
The most important factor. Do you pay your EMIs and credit card bills on time?
- Even one missed payment can significantly drop your score
- Consistent on-time payments over time build a strong score
- Payments more than 30 days late are reported to bureaus

### 2. Credit Utilisation (30% weightage)
How much of your available credit limit are you using?
- Formula: (Total credit used ÷ Total credit limit) × 100
- Example: If your credit card limit is ₹1,00,000 and you've spent ₹40,000, your utilisation is 40%
- Ideal: Keep utilisation below 30%
- High utilisation signals financial stress to lenders

### 3. Length of Credit History (15% weightage)
How long have you had credit accounts?
- Older accounts positively impact your score
- Closing your oldest credit card can hurt your score
- The average age of all your accounts matters

### 4. Credit Mix (10% weightage)
Do you have a healthy mix of credit types?
- Secured loans: home loan, car loan (backed by collateral)
- Unsecured loans: personal loan, credit cards
- Having a mix shows you can manage different types of credit responsibly

### 5. New Credit Enquiries (10% weightage)
Every time you apply for a loan or credit card, the lender does a "hard enquiry" on your credit report. This temporarily lowers your score by a few points.
- Multiple applications in a short time signal financial desperation
- Space out your credit applications
- Checking your own score is a "soft enquiry" and does NOT affect your score

## Common Myths About Credit Scores

**Myth: Checking my own credit score will lower it.**
False. Checking your own score is a soft enquiry and has zero impact on your score.

**Myth: A higher income means a higher credit score.**
False. Your income is not a factor in your credit score. A low-income person with perfect payment history can have a higher score than a high-income person who misses payments.

**Myth: Closing a credit card improves your score.**
Often false. Closing a card reduces your total available credit limit, which increases your utilisation ratio and can lower your score. It also reduces your average credit age.

**Myth: You only have one credit score.**
False. You have four scores — one from each bureau. They will be similar but not identical.

## How to Check Your Credit Score for Free
Under RBI regulations, every Indian is entitled to one free credit report per year from each bureau.
- CIBIL: www.cibil.com
- Experian: www.experian.in
- CRIF: www.crifhighmark.com
- Equifax: www.equifax.co.in

Several apps also offer free score checks: Bajaj Finserv, BankBazaar, Paisabazaar, CRED, OneScore, Bachatt.
